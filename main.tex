\begin{abstract}
The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) is a critical component of the global climate system whose potential collapse constitutes one of the most consequential climate tipping points. Quantifying AMOC resilience, that is, its capacity to maintain the active circulation state under external perturbations, is therefore of high scientific and societal importance. Resilience is a multifaceted concept, encompassing notions such as local and global recovery times, basin volume, and the minimal perturbation required to trigger a collapse. Whether these distinct metrics yield consistent, and therefore trustworthy, signals across models of different complexity has not been systematically examined. Here, we address this question by analysing four models that span a hierarchy in physical complexity: (i) an analytically tractable 3-box salinity model, parameterised using comprehensive model output; (ii) a two-dimensional latitude-depth Boussinesq model of the overturning circulation; (iii) the Earth system model of intermediate complexity CLIMBER-X; and (iv) the general circulation model PlaSim-LSG. Across the hierarchy, we compute model-specific realisations of four complementary resilience concepts: local linear stability, mean global convergence time, basin volume, and minimal critical shock. Our results suggest that not all metrics are coherent across the model hierarchy, as exhibited by incompatible trends under increasing \CO{} forcing in each model. Linear stability constitutes an exception, with consistent resilience loss under this notion in the future climate. These findings have direct implications for the detectability of an imminent AMOC collapse from observational or model-based early-warning signals.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation, tipping points, resilience, model hierarchy

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation is one of the dominant features of the global ocean, transporting large amounts of warm, saline surface waters northward across the Atlantic and returning cold, deep North Atlantic water southward at depth. This large-scale transport pattern underpins the relatively mild climate of Western Europe and plays a central role in the global carbon and heat cycles. Palaeoclimate records document repeated rapid transitions between an active (AMOC-on) and a weakened or collapsed (AMOC-off) state during the Pleistocene, associated with Dansgaard–Oeschger events. Under continued anthropogenic forcing, rising sea surface temperatures, freshening of the North Atlantic from increased precipitation, and Greenland ice-sheet melt may push the AMOC towards a similar transition.

More concretely, in background climate configurations where at least two stable equilibria coexist (the on and off states), the system can be permanently tipped from one to the other either by crossing a critical threshold leading to the annihilation of the formerly stable state (bifurcation-induced tipping) or by a sufficiently large state perturbation (shock-induced tipping) \citep{Ashwin2012tipTypes, Alkhayuon2019AMOCRateTipping}. In the bifurcation-theory framework for AMOC tipping, the transition to the off state is often conceptualised as a saddle-node bifurcation \citep{VanWesten2025SaddleNodeAMOC}. A shock-induced transition, on the other hand, can be understood as an exit of the system state from the basin of attraction associated with the AMOC on state. In both cases, the AMOC is treated as an autonomous dynamical system, potentially influenced by slow parameter changes or external perturbations, such as noise. This framework allows for the characterisation of the system in terms of several quantities, subsumed under the term "resilience" \citep{Krakovska2024ResilienceDynamicalSystems}.

One important notion of resilience has been applied to the AMOC in the context of a bifurcation-induced transition. As the system approaches such a transition through a gradual change in the background climate, the stable on-state loses linear stability, a phenomenon known as critical slowing down (CSD) \citep{Scheffer2009EWSNature}. CSD manifests in observable statistics: near the bifurcation, the dominant eigenvalue of the linearised system approaches zero, leading to increases in variance and autocorrelation of system fluctuations \citep{Held2004CSDDetection, Dakos2008CSDClimate}. These generic signatures have motivated a large body of research on early-warning indicators of tipping \citep{Lenton2011EarlyWarningClimate, Boers2021AMOCEWS, Ditlevsen2023AMOCPrediction}.

Yet CSD-based indicators capture only the system's reaction to small perturbations around the equilibrium, known as local resilience, quantifying how the on-state changes locally as the bifurcation is approached. A complementary perspective is provided by non-local resilience concepts, which characterise the size and geometry of the basin of attraction of the on-state and therefore also capture the system's capacity to recover from larger perturbations \citep{Holling1973ResilienceStabilityEcological, Gunderson2000EcologicalResilienceTheory, Menck2013BasinStability}. A comprehensive assessment of the resilience of the AMOC on state should therefore encompass many different notions. In this work, we consider four such choices. The following heuristic definitions explain the interpretation of the respective resilience measure. The concrete implementation and calculation in each model will vary and are explained in Section \ref{sec:methods}.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Local resilience}: capacity of a system to continuously absorb small perturbations to its equilibium state away from the on-state, such as noise.
  \item \textbf{Convergence time}: duration trajectories take to return to the attractor after a large perturbation away from the on-state.
  \item \textbf{Basin volume}: the volume fraction of a reference region of state space that converges to the on-state \citep{Menck2013BasinStability}.
  \item \textbf{Minimal critical shock}: the smallest instantaneous perturbation that moves the system out of the basin of attraction of the on-state \citep{Klinshov2015DistanceThresholdNumerical, Halekotte2020MinimalFatalShocks}.
\end{enumerate}

In natural systems, controlled perturbation experiments are rarely feasible, making direct estimation of non-local resilience quantities from observations difficult. This is especially true for the AMOC, and numerical models, therefore, play an essential role. However, climate models span an enormous range of complexity, from analytically tractable conceptual models to full Earth system models (ESMs), and it is not obvious whether resilience metrics computed in simple models translate to their more complex counterparts, which include many additional feedbacks and degrees of freedom \citep{Stouffer2006InvestigatingCausesAMOC, Schneider2017EarthSystemModeling}. If metrics disagree across the hierarchy, their utility as real-world system characteristics is severely limited. Several studies have examined individual resilience concepts in the context of the AMOC at specific levels of model complexity \citep{Rahmstorf2005AMOCHystersis, Cimatoribus2014AMOCBoxModel, Boers2021AMOCEWS, Ditlevsen2023AMOCPrediction, Jacques-Dumas2024RareEventResilience}, but a systematic, multi-metric comparison across a model hierarchy is lacking.

Our analysis combines four modelling approaches that span many orders of magnitude of model complexity: (i) a conceptual 3-box salinity model \citep{Wood20195BoxModel, Alkhayuon2019AMOCRateTipping}; (ii) a latitude-depth Boussinesq partial differential equation model \citep{Esclapez2026ConstructingEfficientScore}; (iii) the Earth system model of intermediate complexity CLIMBER-X \citep{Willeit2022EarthSystemModel}; and (iv) the coupled general circulation model PlaSim-LSG \citep{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability}. Section~\ref{sec:methods} describes each model and the resilience metrics computed for it. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} synthesises the findings across the hierarchy and discusses implications for research on the AMOC and on early-warning detection of potential tipping.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

We will compute a common set of resilience quantities for each model while respecting the different experimental possibilities at each level of the hierarchy. For this, we integrate ensemble trajectories from perturbed initial conditions in each model. We set up these experiments in a maximally comparable way, by focussing on two dimensions of perturbations: the average salinity of a geographical ocean box in the North Atlantic and of another box in the Tropical Atlantic. Overall salinity is preserved by compensating with an opposite perturbation in the Southern Oceans. Figure \ref{fig:perturbations-overview} illustrates these regions, and their implementation is explained in the respective subsections below. Table \ref{tab:overview} gives an overview of the concrete meaning and estimation routine for each resilience measure across the models. The PlaSim-LSG model consitutes an exception, as we source model runs from \cite{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability} for our analysis. For a coherent selection of perturbations of interest in the remaining three models, we develop an ad-hoc conversion of the non-dimensional salinty units of the Boussinesq model to \psu{}. Initial conditions are then always probed from a rectangular bounded by $\pm2\psu{}$ amplitude perturbations from the equilibrium on state.

Within each model, we repeat the analysis across a global warming forcing coordinate to be specified for each model below. This allows us to compare directional trends of resilience, which are more meaningful across models than absolute values expressed in model-specific units. The resilience of the AMOC on-state is the primary object of interest throughout, though all analyses could also be carried out for the off-state whenever it is present.

\subsection{3-Box salinity model}
\label{sec:methods:boxmodel}

We adopt the 5-box salinity model introduced by \citet{Wood20195BoxModel} and
further reduced to 3 boxes by \citet{Alkhayuon2019AMOCRateTipping}. The original model partitions the
Atlantic–Indo-Pacific basin into five boxes: North Atlantic ($N$),
Tropical Atlantic ($T$), Southern Ocean ($S$), Indo-Pacific ($IP$), and a Deep box ($D$). Because of the exceedingly slow time scales in the dynamics of the Indo-Pacific and Deep Ocean boxes, their state changes can be disregarded for this analysis. From the remaining three boxes, salinity anomalies $S_N$ and $S_T$ (in units of \psu{} offset from 35\psu{}) in the North and Tropical boxes are the dynamical state variables; the remaining
salinity in the Southern Ocean follows these through salt conservation. The exact model equations and parameters are given in Appendix \ref{app:boxmodel}.

\citet{Wood20195BoxModel} calibrated two distinct parameter sets for the model: one for a pre-industrial climate and one for a doubled-\CO{} climate in the FAMOUS Earth System model. The two configurations differ in the prescribed box temperatures, the freshwater fluxes, the box volumes, and the mixing coefficients. To construct a continuum of models along the axis of increasing \CO{} concentrations, we linearly interpolate between the two parameter sets. The experiments cover the range \CO{}$\in[285,480]$ parts per million (ppm) in increments of $30$ ppm\todo{check}, thereby following the on-state from the pre-industrial configuration towards the doubled-\CO{} configuration. We find that the on-state vanishes in a bifurcation when extrapolating the linear parameter ramp, at around $\CO{}\approx 900\,\text{ppm}$.

All numerical experiments and resilience calculations are performed using the Attractors.jl package \citep{Datseris2023FrameworkGlobalStability} within the DynamicalSystems.jl ecosystem. This computational framework allows for the efficient integration of large initial condition ensembles and the simultaneous tracking of several resilience metrics along a parameter change \citep{Morr2026ComputingResilienceMeasures}. By probing the two-dimensional state space with $10,000$ random initial conditions in the rectangular area $\pm 2\psu{}$ around the on state, we can approximate the desired quantities to high precision. In Figure \ref{fig:box-paper}, we map out the local basin geometry by probing a regular grid of initial conditions and show several example trajectories in terms of AMOC strength and phase space evolution.

\subsection{Boussinesq latitude-depth model}
\label{sec:methods:boussinesq}

The second model is a two-dimensional latitude-depth Boussinesq model of the AMOC. The model evolves temperature, salinity, and vorticity on an $21\times41$ grid of discretised latitude and depth points. The one-dimensional sea surface is forced by cosine profiles of temperature and freshwater forcing, with lower temperatures and less freshwater at the poles. This model is much less expensive than global circulation models but already contains spatially structured salinity fields and overturning, making it a useful intermediate step between the conceptual box model and the three-dimensional climate models.

We introduce a single external forcing parameter that mimics Arctic amplification: increasing it flattens both the equator-to-pole surface temperature profile (Arctic amplification) and freshwater forcing profile (intensification of the hydrological cycle). For the derivation of this parameter, its mapping to \CO{}, and the exact model equations, see Appendix \ref{app:boussinesq}. The perturbation experiments are performed across nine uniformly spaced forcing values, corresponding to \CO{} concentrations from $280\,\text{ppm}$ to approximately $490\,\text{ppm}$. For each configuration, the model is first spun up twice under a weak hemispheric salinity asymmetry directed toward each of the two poles. This yields strong AMOC on- and off-states, where the off-state is reversed in flow direction. Under symmetric salinity forcing, for which an active and a collapsed overturning state can coexist over part of the forcing range, we obtain the final AMOC on and off reference states. Locating these two equilibria allows each perturbed run to be classified by the attractor it settles to, which in turn defines the basin-volume and convergence-time metrics. The on-state reference additionally fixes the unperturbed AMOC strength.

Initial-condition perturbations are applied to two smooth geographic salinity masks. The North Atlantic mask covers the surface-intensified latitude interval $40°$--$90°\text{N}$, and the Tropical Atlantic mask covers $50°\text{S}$--$40°\text{N}$. Both masks have tanh lateral transitions and are multiplied by a exponential surface-layer decay profile in depth. Perturbations are applied as mask-weighted mean salinity anomalies $(\Delta S_N,\Delta S_T)$ on a $20\times20$ grid ($400$ initial conditions) with both coordinates spanning $\pm2\,\psu{}$ around the position of the on state. A compensating offset is then applied within a third box in the Southern Ocean spanning $90°\text{S}$--$50°\text{S}$, conserving the domain-mean salinity.

For the basin-mapping ensemble, only the salinity field of the on-state equilibrium is carried over into the perturbed initial conditions; the temperature, vorticity, and streamfunction fields are set to zero and left to re-adjust to the perturbed salinity under the model dynamics. This isolates the salinity perturbation as the driver of the response. For the local linear-stability estimate (Table~\ref{tab:overview}), by contrast, the small probing perturbations are applied to the complete equilibrium state, retaining all of its fields, so that the local recovery of the full system is captured in the fitted Jacobian.

Throughout, we report salinities in \psu{} and the overturning strength in Sverdrups; the linear calibrations from the model's non-dimensional units to these physical units, both anchored on the pre-industrial equilibrium, are given in \ref{app:boussinesq}. In Figure \ref{fig:boussinesq-paper}, we plot the trajectories of three exemplary perturbations to the Boussinesq model in terms of the two box salinities and the AMOC strength. \subsection{CLIMBER-X}
\label{sec:methods:climberx}

CLIMBER-X \citep{Willeit2022EarthSystemModel} is a fast Earth system model of intermediate complexity (EMIC) that couples a 3-D ocean general circulation model, an energy-balance atmosphere, a thermodynamic sea-ice model, and land-surface components. The ocean component resolves the full three-dimensional salinity and temperature fields, including the main overturning circulation cells, at a resolution of approximately $5°\times 5°$ with 40 vertical levels. CLIMBER-X is computationally efficient enough to permit multi-millennial integrations, making it well-suited for exploring the state space through moderately sized perturbation ensembles.

We first equilibrate CLIMBER-X at prescribed atmospheric \CO{} concentrations in the interval $285$--$420\,\text{ppm}$ in $15\,\text{ppm}$ increments. Each equilibrium run is integrated for up to $20\,000$ model years from the default pre-industrial restart. After equilibration, the trailing $500$ years of the annual ocean time series are used to store reference values for AMOC strength at $26°\text{N}$ and for the salinity diagnostics in the geographical boxes illustrated in \ref{fig:perturbations-overview}. 

The North Atlantic box spans $37.5°$--$90°\text{N}$ and $72.5°\text{W}$--$20°\text{E}$, the Tropical Atlantic box spans $47.5°\text{S}$--$32.5°\text{N}$ and $72.5°\text{W}$--$20°\text{E}$, and a Southern Ocean box ($90°\text{S}$--$52.5°\text{S}$, all longitudes. All boxes have a depth of 150m, which implicates the top 4 ocean layers.  The perturbation masks are applied with a two-grid-cell horizontal and two-layer vertical cosine taper to ensure a more continuous perturbation in space and avoid large local salinity gradients. The interior cells are boosted so that the volume-weighted mean anomaly over the target box remains exactly equal to the prescribed salinity perturbation. A third compensating perturbation is applied in the Southern Ocean box so that the global ocean salt content in the restart is conserved. 

For our experiments, we apply instantaneous salinity perturbations to these boxes directly to the ocean restart file as salinity anomalies in \psu{}, creating an ensemble of modified initial conditions. The perturbations are again uniformly spread in the rectangular region bounded by $\pm2\psu{}$ around the salinities of the stable on state to give $81$ experiments per \CO{} level. Each perturbed initial condition is integrated forward for $2000$ model years. This model exhibits no AMOC off-state and no AMOC collapse at high \CO{} concentrations. Figure \ref{fig:climberx-paper} shows three trajectories starting from perturbed salinity initial conditions in the specified boxes, similar to the two figures above.

\subsection{PlaSim-LSG}
\label{sec:methods:plasim}

The PlaSim-LSG component of the hierarchy is a coupled climate model of intermediate complexity, comprising a spectral primitive-equation atmosphere (T21, $\approx 5.6°\times 5.6°$, 10 vertical levels) and the Large Scale Geostrophic (LSG) ocean \citep{Maier-Reimer1993MeanCirculationHamburg}, a full 3-D ocean circulation model at $3.5°\times 3.5°$ resolution with 22 vertical levels, coupled to a thermodynamic sea-ice scheme and a hydrological cycle.  With approximately $10^5$ degrees of freedom and a throughput of roughly 700 model years per CPU day, PlaSim-LSG occupies a middle ground between the EMICs and full ESMs.

At $285\,\text{ppm}$ (pre-industrial) and $360\,\text{ppm}$ \CO{}, PlaSim-LSG exhibits a bistable AMOC with an on-state of $\approx 16\Sv{}$ and an off-state (partial collapse in the Subpolar Gyre) of $\approx 2\Sv{}$, both confirmed by 4000-year unforced simulations. \citet{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability} applied the edge-tracking algorithm \citep{Lucarini2017EdgeTracking} to locate the edge state (Melancholia state) of the AMOC in PlaSim-LSG at $285\,\text{ppm}$ and $360\,\text{ppm}$.  The algorithm iteratively bisects pairs of trajectories converging to opposite attractors and tracks the resulting pseudotrajectory along the basin boundary until it converges to the edge state. The edge state is a chaotic saddle characterized by persistent centennial AMOC oscillations ($\approx 120$\,yr period, amplitude $3$–$10\Sv{}$), driven by atmosphere–ice–ocean interactions in the North Atlantic. A complete description of the model configuration, its AMOC bistability, and the edge-tracking procedure is given in \citet{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability}, to which we refer the reader for full methodological details.

From the final iterations of edge tracking at each \CO{} level, we extract pairs of edge tracks: one converging to the on-state and one converging to the off-state, both initialised from near-boundary conditions that closely shadow the edge state before diverging. These trajectories provide a natural set of initial conditions from which resilience-relevant transient behaviour can be quantified: they start close to the basin boundary and thus represent challenging recovery paths for the on-state. In total, we analyse 37 bisection iterations at $285\,\text{ppm}$ and 38 bisection iterations at $360\,\text{ppm}$ \todo{name only those trajectories with a transit}, with two trajectories per iteration. Dedicated long equilibrium simulations of the AMOC-on, AMOC-off, and edge states at each \CO{} level provide the attractor clouds against which these transients are evaluated.

Following \citet{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability}, we project the high-dimensional model trajectories onto a physically interpretable two-dimensional state space spanned by the meridional salinity gradient and the vertical salinity gradient. These variables are derived from an EOF analysis of the Atlantic salinity field from $20\,000$ years of edge-tracking simulations \citep{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability}; they disentangle the on-, off-, and edge-state dynamics more cleanly than the raw AMOC strength alone and permit straightforward inter-model comparisons. The on-, off-, and edge-state regions are well separated in this space, with the edge state characterised by a higher vertical SG and a fresher deep North Atlantic than either attractor. We use the edge-track data and long equilibrium runs from \citet{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability} in the two-dimensional subspace of the EOFs to compute the resilience metrics.

\section{Specifics on the 3-Box salinity model}
\label{app:boxmodel}

The model tracks salinity anomalies $S_N$ and $S_T$ (in \psu{} relative to a reference salinity $S_0 = 35\psu{}$) in the North Atlantic and Tropical Atlantic boxes. The Southern Ocean salinity $S_S$ is not an independent variable; it is determined at each instant by global salt conservation, ensuring that the total salt content across all active boxes is held fixed. The salinities $S_B$ (deep box) and $S_{IP}$ (Indo-Pacific box) appear in the equations as prescribed constants, since the dynamics of those boxes are negligibly slow on the time scales of interest.

The governing equations are
\begin{align}
V_N \frac{\dint S_N}{\dint t}=&\,q\left[\left(\frac{K_N}{q}+\Theta(q)\right)(S_T-S_N)-\Theta(-q)(S_B-S_N)\right]-(F_N+aH)S_0\label{eq: dS_N}\\
V_T \frac{\dint S_T}{\dint t}=&\,q\left[\Theta(q)(\gamma S_S+(1-\gamma)S_{IP}-S_T)-\left(\Theta(-q)-\frac{K_N}{q}\right)(S_N-S_T)\right]\label{eq: dS_T}\\&+K_S(S_S-S_T)-(F_T+bH)S_0\nonumber\\
q=&\,\frac{\lambda(\alpha(T_S-T_0)+\beta(S_N-S_S))}{1+\lambda\alpha\mu}\label{eq: q}
\end{align}
where $V_N$ and $V_T$ are box volumes; $K_N$ and $K_S$ are diffusive mixing coefficients coupling the North Atlantic to the Tropical box and the Southern Ocean to the Tropical box, respectively; $F_N$ and $F_T$ are prescribed freshwater fluxes; and $H$ is a freshwater hosing applied to the North Atlantic box (with coefficients $a$ and $b$ distributing the compensating flux to conserve global salinity). The routing parameter $\gamma \in [0,1]$ sets the fraction of the southward return flow that passes through the Southern Ocean rather than the Indo-Pacific.

The overturning flow $q$ (in $\text{m}^3\,\text{s}^{-1}$) depends on the meridional density contrast between the North Atlantic and Southern Ocean boxes. In Eq.~\eqref{eq: q}, $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are thermal and haline expansion coefficients, $\lambda$ is a hydraulic constant, $\mu$ is a geometric factor, and $T_S$ and $T_0$ are the Southern and reference temperatures, which set the thermally driven density contrast. The Heaviside function $\Theta$ separates the cases of positive and negative AMOC strength in Eqs.~\eqref{eq: dS_N} and \eqref{eq: dS_T}, because the direction of the overturning determines which boxes exchange water. The full system therefore has a discontinuity in its right-hand side at $q = 0$.

All model parameters are taken directly from \citet{Wood20195BoxModel} and \citet{Alkhayuon2019AMOCRateTipping}, who calibrated two distinct parameter sets against the FAMOUS Earth System model: one for the pre-industrial climate ($1\times$\CO{}) and one for a doubled-\CO{} climate ($2\times$\CO{}). The two configurations differ in prescribed box temperatures, freshwater fluxes, box volumes, and mixing coefficients. We linearly interpolate (and extrapolate) between them to construct a continuum of model configurations indexed by \CO{} concentration, as described in Section~\ref{sec:methods:boxmodel}.

\section{Specifics on the Boussinesq model}
\label{app:boussinesq}

\subsection*{Physical setup}

The model represents a two-dimensional meridional cross-section of the ocean at non-dimensional latitude $x \in [0, 5]$ and depth $z \in [0, 1]$, where $x = 0$ corresponds to the South Pole, $x = 2.5$ to the equator, and $x = 5$ to the North Pole; $z = 0$ is the ocean floor and $z = 1$ is the surface \citep{Esclapez2026ConstructingEfficientScore}. The flow is assumed two-dimensional and incompressible under the Boussinesq approximation, in which density variations are retained only in the buoyancy term. The velocity field is derived from a streamfunction $\psi(x,z,t)$ via
\begin{equation}
  u = \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial z}, \qquad w = -\frac{\partial \psi}{\partial x},
  \label{eq:vel_bous}
\end{equation}
where $u$ is the horizontal (meridional) velocity and $w$ is the vertical velocity. The vorticity is $\omega = -\nabla^2\psi$. The non-dimensional governing parameters and their default values used in all simulations are given in Table~\ref{tab:boussinesq-params}.

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption{Default non-dimensional parameters for the Boussinesq model.}
  \label{tab:boussinesq-params}
  \begin{tabular}{lll}
    \toprule
    Symbol & Value & Physical meaning \\
    \midrule
    $\mathrm{Pr}$   & $1$              & Prandtl number ($\nu/\kappa_T$) \\
    $\mathrm{Le}$   & $1$              & Lewis number ($\kappa_T/\kappa_S$) \\
    $\mathrm{Ra}$   & $4\times10^4$    & Rayleigh number \\
    $a$             & $5$              & Domain length (pole-to-pole) \\
    $\delta$        & $0.05$           & Surface boundary-layer thickness \\
    $\tau_T$        & $0.1$            & Thermal restoring time scale \\
    $\tau_S$        & $1.0$            & Salinity flux time scale \\
    $\beta_s$       & $0.1$            & Salinity hemispheric asymmetry \\
    $M,\,N$         & $20,\,40$        & Latitude $\times$ depth grid points \\
    $\Delta t$      & $10^{-3}$        & Time step \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection*{Governing equations}

Temperature $T$, salinity $S$, and vorticity $\omega$ evolve according to coupled advection–diffusion equations with surface forcing:
\begin{align}
  \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} + u\frac{\partial T}{\partial x} + w\frac{\partial T}{\partial z}
    &= \nabla^2 T + \frac{h(z)}{\tau_T}\bigl(T_s^\gamma(x) - T\bigr), \label{eq:bous_T}\\[4pt]
  \frac{\partial S}{\partial t} + u\frac{\partial S}{\partial x} + w\frac{\partial S}{\partial z}
    &= \frac{1}{\mathrm{Le}}\nabla^2 S + \frac{h(z)}{\tau_S}\,S_s(x), \label{eq:bous_S}\\[4pt]
  \frac{\partial \omega}{\partial t} + u\frac{\partial \omega}{\partial x} + w\frac{\partial \omega}{\partial z}
    &= \mathrm{Pr}\,\nabla^2\omega + \mathrm{Pr}\cdot\mathrm{Ra}\,\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(T - S), \label{eq:bous_omega}\\[4pt]
  \nabla^2\psi &= -\omega. \label{eq:bous_psi}
\end{align}

The surface boundary-layer profile $h(z) = \exp(-(1-z)/\delta)$ confines the surface forcing to the uppermost ocean. The temperature equation relaxes towards the prescribed surface profile with time scale $\tau_T$; a smaller $\tau_T$ enforces temperature more strongly towards $T_s$. For salinity, a fixed flux formulation is used instead of a relaxation, which is physically appropriate since ocean salinity is set by freshwater exchange with the atmosphere rather than by direct relaxation to a reference profile. A global salinity correction is applied at each time step to conserve domain-mean salinity exactly.

The surface temperature profile is
\begin{equation}
  T_s(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left[\cos\!\left(\tfrac{2\pi x}{a} - \pi\right) + 1\right],
  \label{eq:Ts}
\end{equation}
giving $T_s = 0$ at both poles and $T_s = 1$ at the equator. This equator-to-pole contrast drives the horizontal density gradient that sustains the overturning. The surface salinity flux is
\begin{equation}
  S_s(x) = 3.5\cos\!\left(\tfrac{2\pi x}{a} - \pi\right) - \beta_s\sin\!\left(\tfrac{\pi x}{a} - \tfrac{\pi}{2}\right),
  \label{eq:Ss}
\end{equation}
where the dominant cosine term represents the atmospheric evaporation–precipitation cycle (net evaporation in the tropics, net precipitation at high latitudes) and the $\beta_s$ term introduces a weak hemispheric asymmetry that adds a slight freshwater excess in the northern high latitudes relative to the south, physically motivated by the greater land area and different hydrological cycle of the Northern Hemisphere.

The vorticity equation~\eqref{eq:bous_omega} is obtained by taking the curl of the momentum equation. The baroclinic production term $\mathrm{Pr}\cdot\mathrm{Ra}\,\partial_x(T-S)$ encodes the linearised equation of state under the Boussinesq approximation: density anomalies $\rho \propto -(T - S)$ create horizontal density contrasts that drive overturning vorticity. The product $\mathrm{Pr}\cdot\mathrm{Ra} = 4\times10^4$ governs the balance between advective and diffusive vorticity transport and is the primary control on whether a sustained circulation develops.

\subsection*{Boundary conditions and numerical discretisation}

No-flux ($\partial_n T = \partial_n S = 0$) and no-slip conditions are imposed on all lateral boundaries. The streamfunction satisfies $\psi = 0$ on all walls, consistent with a closed domain with no net overturning flux through any boundary. The depth coordinate is discretised on a non-uniform grid that concentrates points near the surface and the ocean floor via the mapping
\begin{equation}
  z_j = \tfrac{1}{2} + \frac{\tanh\!\bigl(3(j/N - \tfrac{1}{2})\bigr)}{2\tanh(3/2)}, \qquad j = 0,\ldots,N,
  \label{eq:zgrid}
\end{equation}
and the latitude coordinate uses a uniform grid. Spatial derivatives are approximated by second-order finite differences. Equations~\eqref{eq:bous_T}--\eqref{eq:bous_omega} are integrated using an implicit-explicit (IMEX) scheme with time step $\Delta t = 10^{-3}$: diffusion and surface-restoring terms are treated implicitly to avoid the diffusive CFL restriction, while advection terms are treated explicitly. The implicit step at each time leads to a separable Sylvester equation that is solved with the Bartels--Stewart algorithm.

\subsection*{Arctic amplification and \CO{} mapping}

To sweep the model across different climate states, the surface temperature forcing is modified to mimic Arctic amplification by blending the standard profile towards its spatial mean:
\begin{equation}
  T_s^\gamma(x) = (1-\gamma)\,T_s(x) + \tfrac{\gamma}{2},
  \label{eq:Tgamma}
\end{equation}
where $\overline{T_s} = 1/2$ is the domain-mean surface temperature and $\gamma \in [0,1]$ measures the degree of Arctic amplification. At $\gamma = 0$ the forcing is unchanged (pre-industrial); increasing $\gamma$ reduces the equator-to-pole temperature gradient, weakening the baroclinic torque in \eqref{eq:bous_omega} and thereby reducing the thermal driving of the overturning.

The same parameter also intensifies the hydrological forcing: under a warming climate the evaporation--precipitation contrast strengthens (``wet-gets-wetter''), which we represent by scaling the symmetric component of the surface salinity flux \eqref{eq:Ss} while leaving the hemispheric asymmetry unchanged,
\begin{equation}
  S_s^\gamma(x) = (1 + \alpha_s\gamma)\,3.5\cos\!\left(\tfrac{2\pi x}{a} - \pi\right) - \beta_s\sin\!\left(\tfrac{\pi x}{a} - \tfrac{\pi}{2}\right),
  \label{eq:Sgamma}
\end{equation}
with hydrological-intensification coefficient $\alpha_s = 1$, so that the evaporation--precipitation amplitude would double at $\gamma = 1$ and increases by up to $\sim\!8\%$ across the sweep ($\gamma \le 0.08$). Increasing $\gamma$ thus simultaneously weakens the thermal driving and strengthens the salinity forcing of the overturning.

A physically grounded mapping between $\gamma$ and atmospheric \CO{} concentration $C$ is derived from the logarithmic forcing formula and an estimate of polar ocean-surface amplification:
\begin{equation}
  \gamma(C) = \frac{(\alpha_\text{sst}-1)\,\text{ECS}}{\Delta T_0}\log_2\!\left(\frac{C}{C_0}\right)
  = 0.1\log_2\!\left(\frac{C}{280\,\text{ppm}}\right).
  \label{eq:co2_gamma}
\end{equation}
We adopt the IPCC AR6 best-estimate equilibrium climate sensitivity $\text{ECS} = 3.0\,^\circ\text{C}$ per \CO{} doubling \citep{Forster2021IPCCAR6Ch7}, a polar ocean-surface amplification factor $\alpha_\text{sst} = 2.0$ consistent with observed Arctic SST warming being approximately two- to threefold the global-mean increase \citep{Meredith2019IPCCSROCC}, and a pre-industrial Atlantic equator-to-pole SST difference $\Delta T_0 = 30\,^\circ\text{C}$ (approximate Levitus climatology value). These choices give $\gamma = 0$ at pre-industrial \CO{} ($C_0 = 280\,\text{ppm}$) and $\gamma = 0.1$ at doubled \CO{} ($560\,\text{ppm}$). The model sweep covers nine uniformly spaced values of $\gamma \in [0, 0.08]$ (steps of $0.01$), corresponding to \CO{} concentrations from $280\,\text{ppm}$ to approximately $490\,\text{ppm}$.

\subsection*{Perturbation geometry and physical scaling}

Each salinity box is defined by a smooth mask $m_\mathcal{B}(x,z)$ that localises the perturbation while avoiding numerical artefacts from sharp boundaries:
\begin{equation}
  m_\mathcal{B}(x,z) = h(z)\cdot\tfrac{1}{2}\!\left[
    \tanh\!\left(\tfrac{x - x_\mathrm{lo}}{\ell}\right)
    - \tanh\!\left(\tfrac{x - x_\mathrm{hi}}{\ell}\right)
  \right],
  \label{eq:mask}
\end{equation}
with transition half-width $\ell = 0.25$ in non-dimensional latitude units, normalised to have unit peak, i.e.\ $\max_{x,z} m_\mathcal{B}(x,z) = 1$. The North Atlantic box covers $x \in [130/36, 5]$ (approximately $40^\circ$--$90^\circ\text{N}$) and the Tropical Atlantic box covers $x \in [40/36, 130/36]$ (approximately $50^\circ\text{S}$--$40^\circ\text{N}$). A perturbation $(\Delta S_N, \Delta S_T)$ is applied by finding field amplitudes that achieve the target mask-weighted mean shifts simultaneously; the compensating offset that conserves domain-mean salinity is then absorbed by a Southern Ocean box ($x \in [-1, 40/36]$, i.e.\ south of $\sim\!50^\circ\text{S}$).

The reference on-state is obtained from a spin-up that retains the weak hemispheric asymmetry $\beta_s = 0.1$, whereas the perturbed trajectories are integrated under symmetric salinity forcing ($\beta_s = 0$), for which an active and a collapsed overturning state can coexist over part of the $\gamma$ range. At the start of each ensemble, these two $\beta_s=0$ equilibria are identified once per $\gamma$ by releasing the $\beta_s=\pm0.1$ spinup states under symmetric forcing and integrating until they settle, yielding a strong-AMOC and a reversed-AMOC fixed point. Each perturbed run is then terminated as soon as its box salinities enter the $\varepsilon=0.05\,\psu{}$ neighbourhood of either equilibrium; the closer one defines the settled state. Classifying runs in this way yields the basin-volume and convergence-time statistics.

Model salinities are mapped to practical salinity units by a linear calibration $S\,[\psu{}] = 10.09\,s + 26.02$, fixed by requiring the North Atlantic and Tropical boxes of the pre-industrial ($\gamma = 0$) equilibrium to match their observed climatological values of $35.0$ and $36.5\psu{}$; the $\pm2\psu{}$ perturbation grid therefore corresponds to $\pm0.198$ model units. Likewise, the overturning streamfunction is converted to Sverdrups by anchoring the pre-industrial equilibrium value $\psi_0 \approx 1.52$ model units to the RAPID array estimate of $18\Sv{}$, consistent with palaeoclimate reconstructions of the pre-industrial AMOC \citep{Ganachaud2000ImprovedEstimatesGlobal}, giving a linear conversion factor of approximately $11.9\Sv{}/\psi_\text{model}$. Convergence times are converted to year-proxy values by a factor of 100, fixed by matching the pre-industrial mean convergence time ($\approx 1.49$ model units) to centennial-scale relaxation times found in full ocean GCMs; all trends with $\gamma$ are unaffected by this single-point calibration.
