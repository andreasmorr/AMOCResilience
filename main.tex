\begin{abstract}
The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) is a critical component of the global climate system whose potential collapse constitutes one of the most consequential climate tipping points. Quantifying AMOC resilience, that is, its capacity to maintain the active circulation state under growing greenhouse-gas forcing, is therefore of high scientific and societal importance. Resilience is a multifaceted concept, encompassing notions such as basin stability, the minimal perturbation required to trigger a collapse, and characteristic recovery times. Whether these distinct metrics yield consistent, and therefore trustworthy, signals across models of different complexity has not been systematically examined.

Here, we address this question by analyzing four models that span a hierarchy in physical complexity: (i) an analytically tractable 3-box salinity model, parameterized using comprehensive-model output; (ii) a two-dimensional latitude-depth Boussinesq model of the overturning circulation; (iii) the Earth system model of intermediate complexity CLIMBER-X; and (iv) the general circulation model PlaSim-LSG.

Across the hierarchy, we compute model-specific realizations of four complementary resilience concepts: local linear stability, convergence time, basin geometry or basin stability, and the distance from the on-state to the effective stability boundary. \todo{Summarise main findings once results are available.} Our results suggest that \todo{conclusion statement}. These findings have direct implications for the detectability of an imminent AMOC collapse from observational or model-based early-warning signals.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation is one of the dominant features of the global ocean, transporting approximately 18\,Sv of warm, saline surface waters northward across the Atlantic and returning cold, deep North Atlantic Deep Water (NADW) southward at depth \citep{Ganachaud2000ImprovedEstimatesGlobal, Talley2003DataBasedMeridionalOverturning}. This large-scale transport pattern underpins the relatively mild climate of Western Europe and plays a central role in the global carbon and heat cycles \todo{citations}. Palaeoclimate records document repeated rapid transitions between an active (AMOC-on) and a weakened or collapsed (AMOC-off) state during the Pleistocene, associated with Dansgaard–Oeschger events and Heinrich stadials \citep{Lynch-Stieglitz2007AtlanticMeridionalOverturning, McManus2004CollapseRapidResumption}. Under continued anthropogenic forcing, freshening of the North Atlantic from increased precipitation and Greenland ice-sheet melt may push the AMOC towards a similar transition \citep{Rahmstorf2005AMOCHystersis, Caesar2018AMOCFingerprint}.

The theoretical framework underpinning this behavior is that of a saddle-node bifurcation \citep{Stommel1961CircModel, Rahmstorf1995BifurcationsAtlanticThermohaline}.  In regions of parameter space where two stable equilibria coexist (the on and off states), the system can be tipped from one to the other either by crossing a critical parameter threshold leading to the annihilation of the formerly stable state (bifurcation-induced tipping) or by a sufficiently large state perturbation (noise-induced tipping) \citep{Ashwin2012tipTypes, Lohmann2021RiskTippingOverturning}. As the system approaches the saddle-node bifurcation, the stable on-state loses local resilience, a phenomenon generically referred to as critical slowing down (CSD) \citep{Scheffer2009EWSNature}. CSD manifests in observable statistics: near the bifurcation, the dominant eigenvalue of the linearised system approaches zero, leading to increases in variance and autocorrelation of system fluctuations \citep{Held2004CSDDetection, Dakos2008CSDClimate}. These generic signatures have motivated a large body of research on early-warning indicators of tipping \citep{Lenton2011EarlyWarningClimate, Boers2021AMOCEWS, Ditlevsen2023AMOCPrediction}.

Yet CSD-based indicators capture only the reaction of the system to small perturbations around the equilibrium, quantifying how the on-state changes locally as the bifurcation is approached. A complementary perspective is provided by non-local resilience concepts, which characterize the size and geometry of the basin of attraction of the on-state and therefore also capture the system's capacity to recover from larger perturbations \citep{Holling1973ResilienceStabilityEcological, Gunderson2000EcologicalResilienceTheory, Menck2013BasinStability}. A comprehensive assessment of the resilience of the AMOC on state should therefore encompass many different notions. In this work, we consider four such choices. The following heuristic definitions explain the interpretation of the respective resilience measure. The concrete implementation and calculation in our applications will vary and are explained in Section \ref{sec:methods}.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Linear stability}: magnitude of the least stable linear eigenvalue defining the locally attracting fixed point of the dynamical system \citep{Held2004CSDDetection, Weinans2019MultAutoCorrPCA, Morr2024KramersMoyalEWS}.
  \item \textbf{Convergence time}: characterizing how rapidly trajectories return to the attractor after a large perturbation, which encodes information about both the local and global geometry of the basin \citep{Schultz2017PotentialsLimitsBasin}.
  \item \textbf{Basin stability}: the volume fraction of a reference region of state space that converges to the on-state, providing a measure of the basin size \citep{Menck2013BasinStability}.
  \item \textbf{Minimal critical shock}: the smallest instantaneous perturbation that moves the system out of the basin of attraction, i.e.~the distance from the on-state to the basin boundary \citep{Klinshov2015DistanceThresholdNumerical, Datseris2023FrameworkGlobalStability}.
\end{enumerate}

In natural systems, controlled perturbation experiments are rarely feasible, making direct estimation of global resilience quantities from observations difficult. Numerical models, therefore, play an essential role. However, climate models span an enormous range of complexity, from analytically tractable conceptual models to full Earth system models (ESMs), and it is not obvious whether resilience metrics computed in simple models translate to their more complex counterparts, which include many additional feedbacks and degrees of freedom \citep{Stouffer2006InvestigatingCausesAMOC, Schneider2017EarthSystemModeling}. This is not a merely academic concern: if metrics disagree across the hierarchy, their utility as real-world system characteristics is severely limited. Several studies have examined individual resilience concepts in the context of the AMOC at specific levels of model complexity \citep{Rahmstorf2005AMOCHystersis, Lenton2008, Cimatoribus2014AMOCBoxModel, Boers2021AMOCEWS, Ditlevsen2023AMOCPrediction, Jacques-Dumas2024RareEventResilience}, but a systematic, multi-metric comparison across a model hierarchy is lacking.

Our analysis combines four modelling approaches that span many orders of magnitude of model complexity: (i) a conceptual 3-box salinity model \citep{Wood20195BoxModel, Alkhayuon2019AMOCRateTipping} which allows exhaustive sampling of the full two-dimensional state space; (ii) a latitude-depth Boussinesq model in which geographically coherent salinity perturbations can be applied over a dense grid of initial conditions; (iii) large-ensemble trajectory simulations in CLIMBER-X, an Earth system model of intermediate complexity \citep{Willeit2022EarthSystemModel} that resolves three-dimensional ocean circulation and atmosphere–ocean feedbacks; and (iv) an analysis of existing transient convergence and equilibrium trajectories in PlaSim-LSG, a coupled general circulation model whose bistable AMOC, edge state, and stability landscape under varying \CO{} have been thoroughly characterized in recent work \citep{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability}. The paper is structured as follows. Section~\ref{sec:methods} describes each model and the resilience metrics computed for it. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the results from each model. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} synthesizes the findings across the hierarchy and discusses implications for AMOC characterization and early-warning detection of potential tipping.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Overview and general strategy}
\label{sec:methods:overview}

Our aim is to compute a common set of resilience concepts for each model while respecting the different experimental possibilities at each level of the hierarchy. Within each model, we repeat the analysis across a forcing coordinate: interpolated \CO{} parameters and freshwater hosing in the 3-box model, Arctic-amplification strength in the Boussinesq model, prescribed atmospheric \CO{} in CLIMBER-X, and the two available \CO{} levels in PlaSim-LSG. This allows us to compare directional trends, which are more meaningful across models than absolute values expressed in model-specific units.

The metrics fall into two broad classes. Non-local metrics require model trajectories starting from many different initial conditions and measure the size, geometry, or effective boundary distance of the basin of attraction. Local metrics are estimated from time series near an equilibrium and capture the statistical characteristics of the linearised dynamics. Local metrics are related to CSD and can, in principle, be estimated from a single long equilibrium or near-equilibrium run. For this reason, they have been the most popular avenue of assessing resilience in complex natural systems. Our ambition to include systematic estimates of non-local resilience concepts is central to our contribution.

All simulations are conducted at a discrete set of model parameter values. The AMOC on-state is the primary object of interest throughout, though all analyses are also reported for the off-state for completeness (see Supplementary Material).

\subsection{3-Box salinity model}
\label{sec:methods:boxmodel}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

\subsubsection{Model equations}

We adopt the 5-box salinity model introduced by \citet{Wood20195BoxModel} and
further reduced to 3 boxes by \citet{Alkhayuon2019AMOCRateTipping}. The original model partitions the
Atlantic–Indo-Pacific basin into five boxes: North Atlantic ($N$),
Tropical Atlantic ($T$), Southern Ocean ($S$), Indo-Pacific ($IP$), and a Deep box ($D$). Because of the exceedingly slow time scales in the dynamics of the Indo-Pacific and Deep Ocean boxes, their state changes can be disregarded for this analysis. From the remaining three boxes, salinity anomalies $S_N$ and $S_T$ (in units of $10^{-2}\,\psu{}$, relative to a mean value of 35\psu{}) in the North and Tropical boxes are the dynamical state variables; the remaining
salinity in the Southern Ocean is slaved to these through salt conservation. The governing
equations are
\begin{align}
V_N \frac{\dint S_N}{\dint t}=&\,q\left[\left(\frac{K_N}{q}+\Theta(q)\right)(S_T-S_N)-\Theta(-q)(S_B-S_N)\right]-(F_N+aH)S_0\label{eq: dS_N}\\
V_T \frac{\dint S_T}{\dint t}=&\,q\left[\Theta(q)(\gamma S_S+(1-\gamma)S_{IP}-S_T)-\left(\Theta(-q)-\frac{K_N}{q}\right)(S_N-S_T)\right]\label{eq: dS_T}\\&+K_S(S_S-S_T)-(F_T+bH)S_0\nonumber\\
q=&\,\frac{\lambda(\alpha(T_S-T_0)+\beta(S_N-S_S))}{1+\lambda\alpha\mu}\label{eq: q}
\end{align}
where $V_N$ and $V_T$ are box volumes, $K_{i}$ are diffusive mixing coefficients, $F_i$ are prescribed freshwater fluxes, and $H$ is a freshwater hosing applied to the North Atlantic box (with a compensating flux applied to the Tropical box to conserve global salinity). The overturning flow $q$ (in $\text{m}^3\,\text{s}^{-1}$) depends on the meridional density contrast between the North and Tropical boxes, and $\alpha$ and $\beta$ in Eq.\eqref{eq: q} are thermal and haline expansion coefficients, and $\lambda$ is a hydraulic constant. The Heaviside function $\Theta$ separates the case of a positive or negative AMOC in Eqs. \eqref{eq: dS_N} and \eqref{eq: dS_T} because the sense of the overturning circulation determines which boxes exchange water. The full system, therefore, has a discontinuity in its right-hand side at $q = 0$.

\citet{Wood20195BoxModel} and \citet{Alkhayuon2019AMOCRateTipping} calibrated two distinct parameter sets $\boldsymbol{\theta}_c$ for the model: one for a pre-industrial climate and one for a doubled-\CO{} climate. The two configurations differ in the prescribed box temperatures, the freshwater fluxes, the box volumes, and the mixing coefficients. To construct a continuum of models along the axis of increasing \CO{} concentrations, we linearly interpolate (and extrapolate) between the two parameter sets:
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\theta}(\tau) = \boldsymbol{\theta}_{1\times}
    + \tau\,\bigl(\boldsymbol{\theta}_{2\times}
                  - \boldsymbol{\theta}_{1\times}\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\tau=0$ denotes the pre-industrial parameter vector and $\tau=1$ denotes the doubled-\CO{} parameter vector. For plotting and synthesis we map this coordinate to an equivalent concentration scale $c(\tau)=280\,\text{ppm}+\tau(560-280)\,\text{ppm}$. The continuation experiments use $\tau\in[0,2.4]$ in increments of $0.05$, thereby following the on-state from the pre-industrial configuration through the doubled-\CO{} configuration and into an extrapolated high-\CO{} regime. In the cached continuation data the on-state branch loses basin support close to the upper end of this range, around $c\approx 900\,\text{ppm}$.

\subsubsection{Resilience computation}

All resilience calculations are performed using the \texttt{Attractors.jl} package \citep{Datseris2023FrameworkGlobalStability} within the \texttt{DynamicalSystems.jl} ecosystem. Attractors are identified via the \texttt{AttractorsViaRecurrences} mapper on a rectangular grid of initial conditions spanning $S_N\in[-1,1]$ and $S_T\in[-0.1,2]$ in salinity-anomaly units, with $101\times106$ cells. The ODE is integrated with a Rosenbrock solver to handle the discontinuity at $q=0$. At each continuation step we use $100$ samples per parameter value to track attractors by global continuation and then compute resilience measures from $10\,000$ additional initial conditions drawn uniformly from the same state-space rectangle. Convergence to a known attractor is defined by $\varepsilon$-proximity in the two-dimensional salinity state space with $\varepsilon=0.01$.

This workflow is run in two complementary ways. First, the \CO{} continuation follows $\boldsymbol{\theta}(\tau)$ from $\tau=0$ to $\tau=2.4$ and computes resilience measures along the warming trajectory. Second, a freshwater-hosing continuation varies $H$ from $0$ to $0.55\Sv{}$ in increments of $0.01\Sv{}$ separately for the $1\times$\CO{} and $2\times$\CO{} parameter vectors. The hosing scan is not used as a direct analogue of future forcing, but as a controlled bifurcation experiment that exposes how the on-basin changes under additional North Atlantic freshwater input.

These experiments provide the following concrete resilience computations:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Linear stability}: local stability of the continued on-attractor, estimated from the linearised return dynamics around the fixed point.
  \item \textbf{Convergence time}: the median time needed for sampled trajectories to reach $\varepsilon$-proximity of the on-attractor.
  \item \textbf{Convergence pace}: the inverse convergence-time measure returned by \texttt{Attractors.jl}, used to separate fast recovery from slow basin return.
  \item \textbf{Basin stability}: the fraction of the $10\,000$ sampled initial conditions that converge to the on-state.
  \item \textbf{Finite-time basin stability}: the fraction of sampled initial conditions that reach the on-attractor within a finite horizon of $1000$ model years.
  \item \textbf{Minimal critical shock}: the shortest state-space distance from the on-attractor to an initial condition assigned to the off-basin.
\end{enumerate}
The trajectory-level 3-box model diagnostics are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:box-paper}, while the cross-model \CO{} synthesis is summarized in Fig.~\ref{fig:synthesis}.

\subsection{Boussinesq latitude-depth model}
\label{sec:methods:boussinesq}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

\subsubsection{Model description}

The second model is a two-dimensional latitude-depth Boussinesq model of the AMOC. The model resolves temperature, salinity, vorticity, and streamfunction on an $(M+1)\times(N+1)$ grid with $M=20$ latitude points and $N=40$ depth points, and is integrated with time step $\Delta t=10^{-3}$. It uses the non-dimensional salinity-forcing asymmetry $\beta=0.1$ and a seven-mode salinity-forcing configuration. This model is much less expensive than CLIMBER-X but already contains spatially structured salinity fields and an overturning streamfunction, making it a useful intermediate step between the conceptual box model and the three-dimensional climate models.

We introduce a single external forcing parameter, denoted $\gamma$, that controls Arctic amplification of the imposed thermal profile. The experiments sweep $\gamma\in[0,0.1]$ in increments of $0.01$. For each value of $\gamma$, the model is spun up for $20\,000$ time steps from the same analytical initial condition to obtain an AMOC-on reference state. This reference state defines both the unperturbed streamfunction strength and the target box salinities used in the convergence criterion.

To connect the model's forcing parameter to an observationally meaningful scale, we derive an explicit mapping between atmospheric \CO{} concentration $C$ and $\gamma$. Arctic amplification reduces the equator-to-pole sea-surface temperature (SST) gradient; $\gamma$ measures this reduction as a fraction of the pre-industrial gradient $\Delta T_0$. Following the chain $C \to \Delta T_\text{global} \to \Delta T_\text{pole} \to \Delta\text{gradient} \to \gamma$, with global warming given by the logarithmic forcing formula $\Delta T_\text{global}=\text{ECS}\log_2(C/C_0)$ and polar SST amplification $\alpha_\text{sst}$, one obtains
\begin{equation}
  \gamma(C) = \frac{(\alpha_\text{sst}-1)\,\text{ECS}}{\Delta T_0}\log_2\!\left(\frac{C}{C_0}\right).
  \label{eq:co2_gamma}
\end{equation}
We adopt the IPCC AR6 best-estimate equilibrium climate sensitivity $\text{ECS}=3.0\,^{\circ}\text{C}$ per CO$_2$ doubling \citep{Forster2021IPCCAR6Ch7}, a polar ocean-surface amplification factor $\alpha_\text{sst}=2.0$ consistent with observations of Arctic SST warming being approximately two- to threefold the global-mean SST increase \citep{Meredith2019IPCCSROCC}, and a pre-industrial Atlantic equator-to-pole SST difference $\Delta T_0=30\,^{\circ}\text{C}$ (approximate Levitus climatology value). These choices yield the compact formula $\gamma(C)=0.1\log_2(C/280\,\text{ppm})$, so that $\gamma=0$ corresponds to pre-industrial \CO{} ($C_0=280\,\text{ppm}$) and $\gamma=0.1$ to a doubling of \CO{} ($C=560\,\text{ppm}$). Key benchmarks are: Keeling-curve start (1958, $315\,\text{ppm}$) $\to \gamma\approx0.017$; first IPCC report (1990, $354\,\text{ppm}$) $\to \gamma\approx0.033$; present day ($\sim$2023, $420\,\text{ppm}$) $\to \gamma\approx0.059$. The model sweep $\gamma\in[0,0.1]$ therefore spans the range from pre-industrial to doubled-\CO{} conditions, with the present-day forcing level falling near the upper half of the sweep.

\subsubsection{Resilience computation}

Initial-condition perturbations are applied to two smooth geographic salinity masks. The North Atlantic mask covers the surface-intensified latitude interval $x\in[130/36,5]$, corresponding approximately to $40°$--$90°\text{N}$, and the Tropical Atlantic mask covers $x\in[40/36,130/36]$, corresponding approximately to $50°\text{S}$--$40°\text{N}$. Both masks have tanh lateral transitions with half-width $0.25$ in model latitude coordinates and are multiplied by the model's surface-layer decay profile in depth. Perturbations are applied as amplitudes $(\Delta s_\text{north},\Delta s_\text{trop})$ on a $15\times15$ grid with both coordinates spanning $[-0.75,0.75]$.

For each grid point and each $\gamma$, the local anomaly is rescaled so that the mask-weighted box-mean salinity change equals the requested amplitude. A uniform compensating offset is then applied over the full domain so that the domain-mean salinity is exactly conserved. This mirrors the salt-budget constraint imposed in the CLIMBER-X experiments while remaining native to the Boussinesq model.

Each perturbed run is integrated for at most $500\,000$ time steps. The integration is checked every $200$ time steps and is declared converged when the Euclidean distance between the current North/Tropical box-salinity vector and the unperturbed reference vector falls below $\varepsilon=10^{-3}$. Runs are also flagged if any grid point develops negative salinity. The stored quantities are the final overturning streamfunction, the convergence time in model time units, the North and Tropical box-salinity time series, and a categorical basin label based on the final AMOC strength. This produces $225$ perturbed trajectories for each of the $11$ Arctic-amplification values, or $2475$ perturbed integrations in total, with all scalar summaries cached to disk.

\subsection{CLIMBER-X}
\label{sec:methods:climberx}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

\subsubsection{Model description}

CLIMBER-X \citep{Willeit2022EarthSystemModel} is a fast Earth system model of intermediate complexity (EMIC) that couples a 3-D ocean general circulation model, an energy-balance atmosphere, a thermodynamic sea-ice model, and land-surface components.  The ocean component resolves the full three-dimensional salinity and temperature fields, including the main overturning circulation cells, at a resolution of approximately $5°\times 5°$ with 40 vertical levels. CLIMBER-X is computationally efficient enough to permit multi-millennial integrations, making it well-suited for mapping basins of attraction through large perturbation ensembles.

\subsubsection{Resilience computation}

We first equilibrate CLIMBER-X at prescribed atmospheric \CO{} concentrations. The production workflow is configured for the interval $285$--$360\,\text{ppm}$ in $15\,\text{ppm}$ increments, with the paper figures focusing on the endpoint climates $285\,\text{ppm}$ and $360\,\text{ppm}$. Each equilibrium run is integrated for up to $20\,000$ model years from the default pre-industrial restart. After equilibration, the trailing $500$ years of the annual ocean time series are used to store reference values for AMOC strength at $26°\text{N}$ and for the Wood-box salinity diagnostics.

To map the basin of attraction in a physically meaningful two-dimensional subspace, we apply instantaneous salinity perturbations to two coherent ocean regions that correspond closely to the boxes of the conceptual model above. The full-depth Wood North Atlantic box spans $37.5°$--$90°\text{N}$ and $72.5°\text{W}$--$20°\text{E}$ over the full water column; the Wood Tropical Atlantic box spans $47.5°\text{S}$--$32.5°\text{N}$ and $72.5°\text{W}$--$20°\text{E}$ over the upper $875\,\text{m}$. These grid-snapped boundaries match the native $5°$ CLIMBER-X tracer grid and the corresponding Fortran diagnostics written to \texttt{ocn\_ts.nc}. We also define shallow variants of the same boxes over the upper $150\,\text{m}$, which are used as a sensitivity experiment.

Perturbations are applied directly to the ocean restart file as salinity anomalies in \psu{}, creating an ensemble of modified initial conditions. The primary grid uses
\[
  \Delta S_\text{NA}\in\{-2,-1,-0.2,-0.1,0\}\psu{},\qquad
  \Delta S_\text{Trop}\in\{-1,-0.1,0,0.1,1\}\psu{},
\]
yielding $25$ experiments per \CO{} level and box-depth configuration. The perturbation masks may be applied uniformly or with a two-grid-cell horizontal and two-layer vertical cosine taper. In the tapered case, interior cells are boosted so that the volume-weighted mean anomaly over the target box remains exactly equal to the prescribed $\Delta S$. A third compensating perturbation is applied in a Southern Ocean box ($90°\text{S}$--$52.5°\text{S}$, all longitudes, upper $450\,\text{m}$ for the full-depth experiment) so that the global ocean salt content in the restart is conserved. This avoids an immediate correction by CLIMBER-X's internal salt renormalisation routine.

Each perturbed initial condition is integrated forward for up to $5000$--$10\,000$ model years depending on the grid. The main state variable for classification is \texttt{amoc26N}. A run is classified as AMOC-on if the trailing $500$-year mean is at least $12\Sv{}$, AMOC-off if it is at most $3\Sv{}$, and ambiguous otherwise. For long production runs, an embedded watcher can stop a job early when the trailing $200$-year standard deviation of AMOC strength falls below $0.5\Sv{}$ and the mean lies clearly in either the on- or off-state range. The modified CLIMBER-X output additionally writes a year-$0$ instantaneous record to \texttt{ocn\_ts.nc}, so the initial box salinity anomaly can be verified directly from the model output before any dynamical adjustment has occurred.

From the ensemble, we extract quantities analogous to those computed for the conceptual model:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Linear stability}: a $2\times2$ Jacobian in the $(s_\text{woodNA},s_\text{woodTrop})$ plane, estimated from four short perturbation experiments with $\pm0.05\psu{}$ anomalies in each Wood box.
  \item \textbf{Convergence time}: the first year at which the AMOC time series satisfies the convergence criterion, or the final integration time if convergence is not detected.
  \item \textbf{Basin stability}: the fraction of classified grid points in the sampled perturbation rectangle that return to the AMOC-on state.
  \item \textbf{Minimal critical shock}: the smallest Euclidean distance in $(\Delta S_\text{NA},\Delta S_\text{Trop})$ space between the unperturbed equilibrium and a grid point classified as AMOC-off, with the basin map used to localize the effective stability boundary.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{PlaSim-LSG}
\label{sec:methods:plasim}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

\subsubsection{Model and simulation data}

The PlaSim-LSG component of the hierarchy is a coupled climate model of intermediate complexity \todo{Ref}, comprising a spectral primitive-equation atmosphere (T21, $\approx 5.6°\times 5.6°$, 10 vertical levels) and the Large Scale Geostrophic (LSG) ocean \citep{Maier-Reimer1993MeanCirculationHamburg}, a full 3-D ocean circulation model at $3.5°\times 3.5°$ resolution with 22 vertical levels, coupled to a thermodynamic sea-ice scheme and a hydrological cycle.  With approximately $10^5$ degrees of freedom and a throughput of roughly 700 model years per CPU day, PlaSim-LSG occupies a middle ground between the EMICs and full ESMs.  A complete description of the model configuration, its AMOC bistability, and the edge-tracking procedure is given in \citet{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability}, to which we refer the reader for full methodological details.

At $285\,\text{ppm}$ (pre-industrial) and $360\,\text{ppm}$ \CO{} (approximately present-day conditions), PlaSim-LSG exhibits a bistable AMOC with an on-state of $\approx 16\Sv{}$ and an off-state (partial collapse in the Subpolar Gyre) of $\approx 2\Sv{}$, both confirmed by 4000-year unforced simulations. \citet{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability} applied the edge-tracking algorithm \citep{Lucarini2017EdgeTracking} to locate the edge state (Melancholia state) of the AMOC in PlaSim-LSG at $285\,\text{ppm}$ and $360\,\text{ppm}$.  The algorithm iteratively bisects pairs of trajectories converging to opposite attractors and tracks the resulting pseudotrajectory along the basin boundary until it converges to the edge state. The edge state is a chaotic saddle characterized by persistent centennial AMOC oscillations ($\approx 120$\,yr period, amplitude $3$–$10\Sv{}$), driven by atmosphere–ice–ocean interactions in the North Atlantic.

\subsubsection{Resilience computation}

From the final iterations of edge tracking at each \CO{} level, we extract pairs of \emph{edge tracks}: one converging to the on-state and one converging to the off-state, both initialized from near-boundary conditions that closely shadow the edge state before diverging. These trajectories provide a natural set of initial conditions from which resilience-relevant transient behavior can be quantified: they start close to the basin boundary and thus represent challenging recovery paths for the on-state. In total, we analyze 37 bisection iterations at $285\,\text{ppm}$ and 38 bisection iterations at $360\,\text{ppm}$, with two trajectories per iteration. Dedicated long equilibrium simulations of the AMOC-on, AMOC-off, and edge states at each \CO{} level provide the attractor clouds against which these transients are evaluated.

Following \citet{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability}, we project the high-dimensional model trajectories onto a physically interpretable three-dimensional state space spanned by:

\begin{itemize}
  \item the \emph{meridional salinity gradient} (SG$_\text{mer}$): the mean salinity difference between the tropical ($0°$–$20°\text{N}$) and subpolar ($40°$–$80°\text{N}$) Atlantic in the upper $1000\,\text{m}$ (omitting the top $100\,\text{m}$);
  \item the \emph{vertical salinity gradient} (SG$_\text{vert}$): the mean salinity difference between the depth ranges $100$–$1000\,\text{m}$ and $1000$–$3000\,\text{m}$ in the North Atlantic ($46°$–$66°\text{N}$);
  \item the \emph{deep North Atlantic salinity anomaly} ($S'_\text{deep}$): the mean salinity anomaly relative to $35\,\text{g\,kg}^{-1}$ in the Atlantic basin north of $50°\text{N}$, below $1000\,\text{m}$ depth.
\end{itemize}

These variables are derived from an EOF analysis of the Atlantic salinity field from $20\,000$ years of edge-tracking simulations \citep{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability}; they disentangle the on-, off-, and edge-state dynamics more cleanly than the raw AMOC strength alone and permit straightforward inter-model comparisons. The on-, off-, and edge-state regions are well separated in this space, with the edge state characterized by a higher vertical SG and a fresher deep North Atlantic than either attractor. For the paper figures and the edge-distance metric, we work primarily in the leading two reduced coordinates, which are also the coordinates in which the covariance ellipses are visualized.

\subsubsection{Resilience metrics}

We use the edge-track data and long equilibrium runs from \citet{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability} to compute the following resilience metrics, analogous to those derived from the box model and CLIMBER-X but adapted to the available PlaSim-LSG data.

\paragraph{Gaussian state regions.} For each \CO{} level and each state (AMOC-on, AMOC-off, edge), we fit a Gaussian distribution to the corresponding equilibrium run in the reduced salinity space. The mean defines the state location and the covariance defines an ellipsoid. Unless otherwise stated, the analysis uses $3\sigma$ ellipsoids, i.e.\ points whose Mahalanobis distance from the state mean is at most three. These ellipsoids replace the fixed-radius $\varepsilon$ balls used in lower-dimensional models and account for the different internal variability of the on-, off-, and edge-state clouds.

\paragraph{Convergence time from the edge.} For each converged edge-track trajectory we define the convergence time $\tau_c$ as the transit time from the last visit inside the edge-state ellipsoid to the first entry into the target attractor ellipsoid. This definition removes the initial shadowing of the edge state and measures the recovery or collapse phase after the trajectory has committed to one side of the basin boundary. Because all tracks start near the basin boundary, the mean convergence time provides a boundary-conditioned recovery-time measure rather than a basin-wide average.

\paragraph{Edge-to-attractor distance.} We define an edge-to-attractor distance as the gap between the surfaces of the fitted edge ellipsoid and the corresponding attractor ellipsoid in the leading reduced salinity coordinates. The distance is set to zero if the ellipsoids overlap. This gives a geometrical proxy for the distance between the attractor cloud and the effective basin boundary in the absence of a full perturbation grid.

\paragraph{Local stability indicators.} From the long equilibrium runs at each \CO{} level we compute CSD-based local stability metrics from the detrended time series of the three reduced-state-space variables:

\begin{description}
  \item[Total variance] The trace of the covariance matrix, measuring the overall spread of fluctuations around the on-state.
  \item[Dominant variance] The largest eigenvalue of the covariance matrix, which captures variance along the least-stable (most nearly critical) direction.
  \item[Lag-1 autocorrelation] $\hat{\rho}(1)$, expected to increase towards unity as the dominant eigenvalue approaches zero.
  \item[Integrated autocorrelation time] $\hat{\tau}_\text{AC} = \tfrac{1}{2} + \sum_{\ell=1}^{L}\hat{\rho}(\ell)$, a more robust persistence estimator, truncated at the first zero crossing of $\hat{\rho}(\ell)$.
\end{description}

All four quantities are computed per reduced-state-space dimension and then averaged to yield scalar summary statistics comparable to the local stability indicators derived from the box model and CLIMBER-X.

\section{CLIMBER-X perturbation regions}
\label{app:regions}

The full-depth CLIMBER-X perturbation regions are grid-snapped versions of the
Wood-box geometry used throughout the paper. The North Atlantic box spans
$37.5°$--$90°\text{N}$ and $72.5°\text{W}$--$20°\text{E}$ over the full
water column. The Tropical Atlantic box spans $47.5°\text{S}$--$32.5°\text{N}$
and $72.5°\text{W}$--$20°\text{E}$ over the upper $875\,\text{m}$. The
Southern compensation box spans $90°\text{S}$--$52.5°\text{S}$ over all
longitudes and the upper $450\,\text{m}$. Shallow sensitivity experiments use
the same horizontal boundaries but restrict all three boxes to the upper
$150\,\text{m}$.

The Python restart-modification code applies the same cell selections as the
Fortran diagnostics in \texttt{ocn\_out.f90}. With tapering enabled, the
horizontal margin is two $5°$ grid cells wide and the bottom taper of
depth-limited boxes spans two vertical layers. All reported perturbation
amplitudes are volume-weighted box-mean anomalies after this taper correction.

\section{Boussinesq perturbation regions}
\label{app:boussinesq_regions}

The Boussinesq perturbation masks are defined in the model's non-dimensional
latitude coordinate $x\in[0,5]$, where $x=0$ corresponds to $90°\text{S}$ and
$x=5$ corresponds to $90°\text{N}$. The North Atlantic mask uses
$x\in[130/36,5]$ and the Tropical Atlantic mask uses $x\in[40/36,130/36]$.
These correspond approximately to $40°$--$90°\text{N}$ and
$50°\text{S}$--$40°\text{N}$, respectively. Both masks are surface-intensified
by the same vertical decay profile that enters the model's salinity forcing,
and both are normalized so that the requested perturbation amplitude equals
the mask-weighted box-mean salinity change.

\section{PlaSim-LSG reduced state space}
\label{app:eof}

The three-dimensional reduced state space used to analyse the
PlaSim-LSG trajectories is defined and justified in full in
\citet{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability} (their Section~III\,D and Fig.~6).  The
variables — meridional salinity gradient SG$_\text{mer}$, vertical
salinity gradient SG$_\text{vert}$, and deep North Atlantic salinity
anomaly $S'_\text{deep}$ — were derived by EOF analysis of the
Atlantic zonally-averaged salinity field from $20\,000$ years of edge-
tracking simulations at $360\,\text{ppm}$.  The leading two EOFs reveal
a meridional salinity dipole in the upper $1000\,\text{m}$ and a
vertical dipole in the North Atlantic, respectively.  The three
variables chosen are linear functions of the full salinity field that
closely align with these dominant modes while remaining physically
interpretable and directly computable from any spatially resolved ocean
model, facilitating the inter-model comparisons pursued in the present
study.
