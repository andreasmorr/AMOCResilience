\begin{abstract}
The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) is a critical component of the global climate system whose potential collapse constitutes one of the most consequential climate tipping points. Quantifying AMOC resilience, that is, its capacity to maintain the active circulation state under external perturbations, is therefore of high scientific and societal importance. Resilience is a multifaceted concept, encompassing notions such as local and global recovery times, basin volume, and the minimal perturbation required to trigger a collapse. Whether these distinct metrics yield consistent, and therefore trustworthy, signals across models of different complexity has not been systematically examined. Here, we address this question by analysing four models that span a hierarchy in physical complexity: (i) an analytically tractable 3-box salinity model, parameterised using comprehensive model output; (ii) a two-dimensional latitude-depth Boussinesq model of the overturning circulation; (iii) the Earth system model of intermediate complexity CLIMBER-X; and (iv) the general circulation model PlaSim-LSG. Across the hierarchy, we compute model-specific realisations of four complementary resilience concepts: local linear stability, median global convergence time, basin volume, and minimal critical shock. Our results suggest that not all metrics are coherent across the model hierarchy, as exhibited by incompatible trends under increasing \CO{} forcing in each model. Linear stability constitutes an exception, with consistent resilience loss under this notion in the future climate. These findings have direct implications for the detectability of an imminent AMOC collapse from observational or model-based early-warning signals.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation, tipping points, resilience, model hierarchy

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation is one of the dominant features of the global ocean, transporting approximately 18\,Sv of warm, saline surface waters northward across the Atlantic and returning cold, deep North Atlantic Deep Water (NADW) southward at depth \citep{Ganachaud2000ImprovedEstimatesGlobal, Talley2003DataBasedMeridionalOverturning}. This large-scale transport pattern underpins the relatively mild climate of Western Europe and plays a central role in the global carbon and heat cycles \todo{citations}. Palaeoclimate records document repeated rapid transitions between an active (AMOC-on) and a weakened or collapsed (AMOC-off) state during the Pleistocene, associated with Dansgaard–Oeschger events and Heinrich stadials \citep{Lynch-Stieglitz2007AtlanticMeridionalOverturning, McManus2004CollapseRapidResumption}. Under continued anthropogenic forcing, freshening of the North Atlantic from increased precipitation and Greenland ice-sheet melt may push the AMOC towards a similar transition \citep{Rahmstorf2005AMOCHystersis}.

In background climate configurations where at least two stable equilibria coexist (the on and off states), the system can be tipped from one to the other either by crossing a critical threshold leading to the annihilation of the formerly stable state (bifurcation-induced tipping) or by a sufficiently large state perturbation (noise-induced tipping) \citep{Ashwin2012tipTypes, Lohmann2021RiskTippingOverturning}. In the bifurcation-theory framework for AMOC tipping, the transition to the off state is often conceptualised as a saddle-node bifurcation \citep{VanWesten2025SaddleNodeAMOC}. As the system approaches such a transition through a gradual change in the background climate, the stable on-state loses linear stability, a phenomenon known as critical slowing down (CSD) \citep{Scheffer2009EWSNature}. CSD manifests in observable statistics: near the bifurcation, the dominant eigenvalue of the linearised system approaches zero, leading to increases in variance and autocorrelation of system fluctuations \citep{Held2004CSDDetection, Dakos2008CSDClimate}. These generic signatures have motivated a large body of research on early-warning indicators of tipping \citep{Lenton2011EarlyWarningClimate, Boers2021AMOCEWS, Ditlevsen2023AMOCPrediction}.

Yet CSD-based indicators capture only the system's reaction to small perturbations around the equilibrium, known as local resilience, quantifying how the on-state changes locally as the bifurcation is approached. A complementary perspective is provided by non-local resilience concepts, which characterise the size and geometry of the basin of attraction of the on-state and therefore also capture the system's capacity to recover from larger perturbations \citep{Holling1973ResilienceStabilityEcological, Gunderson2000EcologicalResilienceTheory, Menck2013BasinStability}. A comprehensive assessment of the resilience of the AMOC on state should therefore encompass many different notions. In this work, we consider four such choices. The following heuristic definitions explain the interpretation of the respective resilience measure. The concrete implementation and calculation in our applications will vary and are explained in Section \ref{sec:methods}.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Linear stability}: magnitude of the least stable linear eigenvalue defining the locally attracting fixed point of the dynamical system \citep{Held2004CSDDetection, Weinans2019MultAutoCorrPCA, Morr2024KramersMoyalEWS}.
  \item \textbf{Convergence time}: characterising how rapidly trajectories return to the attractor after a large perturbation, which encodes information about both the local and global geometry of the basin \citep{Schultz2017PotentialsLimitsBasin}.
  \item \textbf{Basin volume}: the volume fraction of a reference region of state space that converges to the on-state, providing a measure of the basin size \citep{Menck2013BasinStability}.
  \item \textbf{Minimal critical shock}: the smallest instantaneous perturbation that moves the system out of the basin of attraction, i.e.~the distance from the on-state to the basin boundary \citep{Klinshov2015DistanceThresholdNumerical, Datseris2023FrameworkGlobalStability}.
\end{enumerate}

In natural systems, controlled perturbation experiments are rarely feasible, making direct estimation of global resilience quantities from observations difficult. Numerical models, therefore, play an essential role. However, climate models span an enormous range of complexity, from analytically tractable conceptual models to full Earth system models (ESMs), and it is not obvious whether resilience metrics computed in simple models translate to their more complex counterparts, which include many additional feedbacks and degrees of freedom \citep{Stouffer2006InvestigatingCausesAMOC, Schneider2017EarthSystemModeling}. This is not a merely academic concern: if metrics disagree across the hierarchy, their utility as real-world system characteristics is severely limited. Several studies have examined individual resilience concepts in the context of the AMOC at specific levels of model complexity \citep{Rahmstorf2005AMOCHystersis, Cimatoribus2014AMOCBoxModel, Boers2021AMOCEWS, Ditlevsen2023AMOCPrediction, Jacques-Dumas2024RareEventResilience}, but a systematic, multi-metric comparison across a model hierarchy is lacking.

Our analysis combines four modelling approaches that span many orders of magnitude of model complexity: (i) a conceptual 3-box salinity model \citep{Wood20195BoxModel, Alkhayuon2019AMOCRateTipping} which allows exhaustive sampling of the full two-dimensional state space; (ii) a latitude-depth Boussinesq model \citep{Esclapez2026ConstructingEfficientScore} in which geographically coherent salinity perturbations can be applied over a dense grid of initial conditions; (iii) large-ensemble trajectory simulations in CLIMBER-X, an Earth system model of intermediate complexity \citep{Willeit2022EarthSystemModel} that resolves three-dimensional ocean circulation and atmosphere–ocean feedbacks; and (iv) an analysis of existing transient convergence and equilibrium trajectories in PlaSim-LSG, a coupled general circulation model whose bistable AMOC, edge state, and stability landscape under varying \CO{} have been thoroughly characterized in recent work \citep{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability}. The paper is structured as follows. Section~\ref{sec:methods} describes each model and the resilience metrics computed for it. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} synthesizes the findings across the hierarchy and discusses implications for AMOC characterization and early-warning detection of potential tipping.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
% ============================================================

Our aim is to compute a common set of resilience concepts for each model while respecting the different experimental possibilities at each level of the hierarchy. Within each model, we repeat the analysis across a forcing coordinate: interpolated \CO{}-calibrated parameters and freshwater hosing in the 3-box model, Arctic-amplification strength in the Boussinesq model, prescribed atmospheric \CO{} in CLIMBER-X, and the two available \CO{} levels in PlaSim-LSG. This allows us to compare directional trends, which are more meaningful across models than absolute values expressed in model-specific units.

The metrics fall into two broad classes. Non-local metrics require model trajectories starting from many different initial conditions and measure the basin of attraction's size and geometry. The local metric of linear stability is estimated from time series near an equilibrium and captures the characteristics of the linearised dynamics. In stochastically forced systems, local metrics are related to CSD and can, in principle, be estimated from a single long equilibrium or near-equilibrium run. For this reason, they have been the most popular avenue of assessing resilience in complex natural systems. Our ambition to include systematic estimates of non-local resilience concepts is central to our contribution.

All simulations are conducted at a discrete set of model parameter values. The AMOC on-state is the primary object of interest throughout, though all analyses could also be carried out for the off-state whenever it is present.

\subsection{3-Box salinity model}
\label{sec:methods:boxmodel}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

We adopt the 5-box salinity model introduced by \citet{Wood20195BoxModel} and
further reduced to 3 boxes by \citet{Alkhayuon2019AMOCRateTipping}. The original model partitions the
Atlantic–Indo-Pacific basin into five boxes: North Atlantic ($N$),
Tropical Atlantic ($T$), Southern Ocean ($S$), Indo-Pacific ($IP$), and a Deep box ($D$). Because of the exceedingly slow time scales in the dynamics of the Indo-Pacific and Deep Ocean boxes, their state changes can be disregarded for this analysis. From the remaining three boxes, salinity anomalies $S_N$ and $S_T$ (in units of $10^{-2}\,\psu{}$, relative to a mean value of 35\psu{}) in the North and Tropical boxes are the dynamical state variables; the remaining
salinity in the Southern Ocean is slaved to these through salt conservation. The governing
equations are
\begin{align}
V_N \frac{\dint S_N}{\dint t}=&\,q\left[\left(\frac{K_N}{q}+\Theta(q)\right)(S_T-S_N)-\Theta(-q)(S_B-S_N)\right]-(F_N+aH)S_0\label{eq: dS_N}\\
V_T \frac{\dint S_T}{\dint t}=&\,q\left[\Theta(q)(\gamma S_S+(1-\gamma)S_{IP}-S_T)-\left(\Theta(-q)-\frac{K_N}{q}\right)(S_N-S_T)\right]\label{eq: dS_T}\\&+K_S(S_S-S_T)-(F_T+bH)S_0\nonumber\\
q=&\,\frac{\lambda(\alpha(T_S-T_0)+\beta(S_N-S_S))}{1+\lambda\alpha\mu}\label{eq: q}
\end{align}
where $V_N$ and $V_T$ are box volumes, $K_{i}$ are diffusive mixing coefficients, $F_i$ are prescribed freshwater fluxes, and $H$ is a freshwater hosing applied to the North Atlantic box (with a compensating flux applied to the Tropical box to conserve global salinity). The overturning flow $q$ (in $\text{m}^3\,\text{s}^{-1}$) depends on the meridional density contrast between the North and Tropical boxes, and $\alpha$ and $\beta$ in Eq.\eqref{eq: q} are thermal and haline expansion coefficients, and $\lambda$ is a hydraulic constant. The Heaviside function $\Theta$ separates the case of a positive or negative AMOC strength $q$ in Eqs. \eqref{eq: dS_N} and \eqref{eq: dS_T} because the sense of the overturning circulation determines which boxes exchange water. The full system, therefore, has a discontinuity in its right-hand side at $q = 0$.

\citet{Wood20195BoxModel} calibrated two distinct parameter sets $\boldsymbol{\theta}_c$ for the model: one for a pre-industrial climate and one for a doubled-\CO{} climate. The two configurations differ in the prescribed box temperatures, the freshwater fluxes, the box volumes, and the mixing coefficients. To construct a continuum of models along the axis of increasing \CO{} concentrations, we linearly interpolate (and extrapolate) between the two parameter sets:
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\theta}(\tau) = \boldsymbol{\theta}_{1\times}
    + \tau\,\bigl(\boldsymbol{\theta}_{2\times}
                  - \boldsymbol{\theta}_{1\times}\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\tau=0$ denotes the pre-industrial parameter vector and $\tau=1$ denotes the doubled-\CO{} parameter vector. For plotting and synthesis we map this coordinate to an equivalent concentration scale $c(\tau)=280\,\text{ppm}+\tau(560-280)\,\text{ppm}$. The experiments cover the range $\tau\in[0,2.4]$ in increments of $0.05$, thereby following the on-state from the pre-industrial configuration through the doubled-\CO{} configuration and into an extrapolated high-\CO{} regime. We find that the on-state vanishes in a bifurcation close to the upper end of this range, around $c\approx 900\,\text{ppm}$.

All resilience calculations are performed using the \texttt{Attractors.jl} package \citep{Datseris2023FrameworkGlobalStability} within the \texttt{DynamicalSystems.jl} ecosystem. This computational framework allows for the efficient integration of large initial condition ensembles and the simultaneous tracking of several resilience metrics along a parameter change \citep{Morr2026ComputingResilienceMeasures}. By probing the two-dimensional state space with $10,000$ random initial conditions in the rectangular area defined by $(S_N,S_T)\in [25:45,34:55]$psu, we can approximate the desired quantities to high precision. In Figure \ref{fig:box-paper}, we map out the basin geometry by probing a regular grid of initial conditions and show several example trajectories in terms of AMOC strength and phase space evolution. The AMOC on-state of the high-\CO{} configuration is close to vanishing via a bifurcation.

\subsection{Boussinesq latitude-depth model}
\label{sec:methods:boussinesq}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The second model is a two-dimensional latitude-depth Boussinesq model of the AMOC. The model resolves temperature, salinity, vorticity, and streamfunction on an $(M+1)\times(N+1)$ grid with $M=20$ latitude points and $N=40$ depth points, and is integrated with time step $\Delta t=10^{-3}$. It uses the non-dimensional salinity-forcing asymmetry $\beta=0.1$ and a seven-mode salinity-forcing configuration. This model is much less expensive than CLIMBER-X but already contains spatially structured salinity fields and an overturning streamfunction, making it a useful intermediate step between the conceptual box model and the three-dimensional climate models.

We introduce a single external forcing parameter, denoted $\gamma$, that controls Arctic amplification of the imposed thermal profile. The experiments sweep $\gamma\in[0,0.1]$ in increments of $0.01$. For each value of $\gamma$, the model is spun up for $20\,000$ time steps from the same analytical initial condition to obtain an AMOC-on reference state. This reference state defines both the unperturbed streamfunction strength and the target box salinities used in the convergence criterion.

To connect the model's forcing parameter to an observationally meaningful scale, we derive an explicit mapping between atmospheric \CO{} concentration $C$ and $\gamma$. Arctic amplification reduces the equator-to-pole sea-surface temperature (SST) gradient; $\gamma$ measures this reduction as a fraction of the pre-industrial gradient $\Delta T_0$. With global warming given by the logarithmic forcing formula $\Delta T_\text{global}=\text{ECS}\log_2(C/C_0)$ and polar SST amplification $\alpha_\text{sst}$, one obtains
\begin{equation}
  \gamma(C) = \frac{(\alpha_\text{sst}-1)\,\text{ECS}}{\Delta T_0}\log_2\!\left(\frac{C}{C_0}\right).
  \label{eq:co2_gamma}
\end{equation}
We adopt the IPCC AR6 best-estimate equilibrium climate sensitivity $\text{ECS}=3.0\,^{\circ}\text{C}$ per CO$_2$ doubling \citep{Forster2021IPCCAR6Ch7}, a polar ocean-surface amplification factor $\alpha_\text{sst}=2.0$ consistent with observations of Arctic SST warming being approximately two- to threefold the global-mean SST increase \citep{Meredith2019IPCCSROCC}, and a pre-industrial Atlantic equator-to-pole SST difference $\Delta T_0=30\,^{\circ}\text{C}$ (approximate Levitus climatology value). These choices yield the compact formula $\gamma(C)=0.1\log_2(C/280\,\text{ppm})$, so that $\gamma=0$ corresponds to pre-industrial \CO{} ($C_0=280\,\text{ppm}$) and $\gamma=0.1$ to a doubling of \CO{} ($C=560\,\text{ppm}$). Key benchmarks are: Keeling-curve start (1958, $315\,\text{ppm}$) $\to \gamma\approx0.017$; first IPCC report (1990, $354\,\text{ppm}$) $\to \gamma\approx0.033$; present day ($\sim$2023, $420\,\text{ppm}$) $\to \gamma\approx0.059$. The model sweep $\gamma\in[0,0.1]$ therefore spans the range from pre-industrial to doubled-\CO{} conditions, with the present-day forcing level falling near the upper half of the sweep.

Initial-condition perturbations are applied to two smooth geographic salinity masks. The North Atlantic mask covers the surface-intensified latitude interval $x\in[130/36,5]$, corresponding approximately to $40°$--$90°\text{N}$, and the Tropical Atlantic mask covers $x\in[40/36,130/36]$, corresponding approximately to $50°\text{S}$--$40°\text{N}$. Both masks have tanh lateral transitions with half-width $0.25$ in model latitude coordinates and are multiplied by the model's surface-layer decay profile in depth. Perturbations are applied as amplitudes $(\Delta S_N,\Delta S_T)$ on a $15\times15$ grid with both coordinates spanning $[-0.25,0.25]$.

For each grid point and each $\gamma$, the local anomaly is rescaled so that the mask-weighted box-mean salinity change equals the requested amplitude. A uniform compensating offset is then applied over the full domain so that the domain-mean salinity is exactly conserved.

Each perturbed run is integrated for at most $500\,000$ time steps. The integration is checked every $200$ time steps and is declared converged when the Euclidean distance between the current North/Tropical box-salinity vector and the unperturbed reference vector falls below $\varepsilon=10^{-3}$.

\subsection{CLIMBER-X}
\label{sec:methods:climberx}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

CLIMBER-X \citep{Willeit2022EarthSystemModel} is a fast Earth system model of intermediate complexity (EMIC) that couples a 3-D ocean general circulation model, an energy-balance atmosphere, a thermodynamic sea-ice model, and land-surface components.  The ocean component resolves the full three-dimensional salinity and temperature fields, including the main overturning circulation cells, at a resolution of approximately $5°\times 5°$ with 40 vertical levels. CLIMBER-X is computationally efficient enough to permit multi-millennial integrations, making it well-suited for mapping basins of attraction through large perturbation ensembles.

We first equilibrate CLIMBER-X at prescribed atmospheric \CO{} concentrations in the interval $285$--$420\,\text{ppm}$ in $15\,\text{ppm}$ increments. Each equilibrium run is integrated for up to $20\,000$ model years from the default pre-industrial restart. After equilibration, the trailing $500$ years of the annual ocean time series are used to store reference values for AMOC strength at $26°\text{N}$ and for the Wood-box salinity diagnostics.

To map the basin of attraction in a physically meaningful two-dimensional subspace, we apply instantaneous salinity perturbations to two coherent ocean regions that correspond closely to the boxes of the conceptual model above. The full-depth Wood North Atlantic box spans $37.5°$--$90°\text{N}$ and $72.5°\text{W}$--$20°\text{E}$ over the full water column; the Wood Tropical Atlantic box spans $47.5°\text{S}$--$32.5°\text{N}$ and $72.5°\text{W}$--$20°\text{E}$ over the upper $875\,\text{m}$. These grid-snapped boundaries match the native $5°$ CLIMBER-X tracer grid and the corresponding Fortran diagnostics written to \texttt{ocn\_ts.nc}. We also define shallow variants of the same boxes over the upper $150\,\text{m}$, which are used as a sensitivity experiment.

Perturbations are applied directly to the ocean restart file as salinity anomalies in \psu{}, creating an ensemble of modified initial conditions. The primary grid uses
\[
  \Delta S_\text{N}\in\{-2,-1,-0.2,-0.1,0\}\psu{},\qquad
  \Delta S_\text{T}\in\{-1,-0.1,0,0.1,1\}\psu{},
\]
yielding $25$ experiments per \CO{} level and box-depth configuration. The perturbation masks are applied with a two-grid-cell horizontal and two-layer vertical cosine taper. The interior cells are boosted so that the volume-weighted mean anomaly over the target box remains exactly equal to the prescribed $\Delta S$. A third compensating perturbation is applied in a Southern Ocean box ($90°\text{S}$--$52.5°\text{S}$, all longitudes, upper $450\,\text{m}$ for the full-depth experiment) so that the global ocean salt content in the restart is conserved. Each perturbed initial condition is integrated forward for $2000$ model years. This model exhibits no AMOC off-state.

\subsection{PlaSim-LSG}
\label{sec:methods:plasim}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The PlaSim-LSG component of the hierarchy is a coupled climate model of intermediate complexity, comprising a spectral primitive-equation atmosphere (T21, $\approx 5.6°\times 5.6°$, 10 vertical levels) and the Large Scale Geostrophic (LSG) ocean \citep{Maier-Reimer1993MeanCirculationHamburg}, a full 3-D ocean circulation model at $3.5°\times 3.5°$ resolution with 22 vertical levels, coupled to a thermodynamic sea-ice scheme and a hydrological cycle.  With approximately $10^5$ degrees of freedom and a throughput of roughly 700 model years per CPU day, PlaSim-LSG occupies a middle ground between the EMICs and full ESMs. A complete description of the model configuration, its AMOC bistability, and the edge-tracking procedure is given in \citet{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability}, to which we refer the reader for full methodological details.

At $285\,\text{ppm}$ (pre-industrial) and $360\,\text{ppm}$ \CO{}, PlaSim-LSG exhibits a bistable AMOC with an on-state of $\approx 16\Sv{}$ and an off-state (partial collapse in the Subpolar Gyre) of $\approx 2\Sv{}$, both confirmed by 4000-year unforced simulations. \citet{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability} applied the edge-tracking algorithm \citep{Lucarini2017EdgeTracking} to locate the edge state (Melancholia state) of the AMOC in PlaSim-LSG at $285\,\text{ppm}$ and $360\,\text{ppm}$.  The algorithm iteratively bisects pairs of trajectories converging to opposite attractors and tracks the resulting pseudotrajectory along the basin boundary until it converges to the edge state. The edge state is a chaotic saddle characterized by persistent centennial AMOC oscillations ($\approx 120$\,yr period, amplitude $3$–$10\Sv{}$), driven by atmosphere–ice–ocean interactions in the North Atlantic.

From the final iterations of edge tracking at each \CO{} level, we extract pairs of edge tracks: one converging to the on-state and one converging to the off-state, both initialised from near-boundary conditions that closely shadow the edge state before diverging. These trajectories provide a natural set of initial conditions from which resilience-relevant transient behaviour can be quantified: they start close to the basin boundary and thus represent challenging recovery paths for the on-state. In total, we analyse 37 bisection iterations at $285\,\text{ppm}$ and 38 bisection iterations at $360\,\text{ppm}$, with two trajectories per iteration. Dedicated long equilibrium simulations of the AMOC-on, AMOC-off, and edge states at each \CO{} level provide the attractor clouds against which these transients are evaluated.

Following \citet{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability}, we project the high-dimensional model trajectories onto a physically interpretable two-dimensional state space spanned by:

\begin{itemize}
  \item the \emph{meridional salinity gradient} (SG$_\text{mer}$): the mean salinity difference between the tropical ($0°$–$20°\text{N}$) and subpolar ($40°$–$80°\text{N}$) Atlantic in the upper $1000\,\text{m}$ (omitting the top $100\,\text{m}$);
  \item the \emph{vertical salinity gradient} (SG$_\text{vert}$): the mean salinity difference between the depth ranges $100$–$1000\,\text{m}$ and $1000$–$3000\,\text{m}$ in the North Atlantic ($46°$–$66°\text{N}$);
\end{itemize}

These variables are derived from an EOF analysis of the Atlantic salinity field from $20\,000$ years of edge-tracking simulations \citep{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability}; they disentangle the on-, off-, and edge-state dynamics more cleanly than the raw AMOC strength alone and permit straightforward inter-model comparisons. The on-, off-, and edge-state regions are well separated in this space, with the edge state characterised by a higher vertical SG and a fresher deep North Atlantic than either attractor.

We use the edge-track data and long equilibrium runs from \citet{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability} to compute the resilience metrics, analogous to those derived from the box model and CLIMBER-X but adapted to the available PlaSim-LSG data.

\section{CLIMBER-X perturbation regions}
\label{app:regions}

The full-depth CLIMBER-X perturbation regions are grid-snapped versions of the
Wood-box geometry used throughout the paper. The North Atlantic box spans
$37.5°$--$90°\text{N}$ and $72.5°\text{W}$--$20°\text{E}$ over the full
water column. The Tropical Atlantic box spans $47.5°\text{S}$--$32.5°\text{N}$
and $72.5°\text{W}$--$20°\text{E}$ over the upper $875\,\text{m}$. The
Southern compensation box spans $90°\text{S}$--$52.5°\text{S}$ over all
longitudes and the upper $450\,\text{m}$. Shallow sensitivity experiments use
the same horizontal boundaries but restrict all three boxes to the upper
$150\,\text{m}$.

The Python restart-modification code applies the same cell selections as the
Fortran diagnostics in \texttt{ocn\_out.f90}. With tapering enabled, the
horizontal margin is two $5°$ grid cells wide and the bottom taper of
depth-limited boxes spans two vertical layers. All reported perturbation
amplitudes are volume-weighted box-mean anomalies after this taper correction.

\section{Boussinesq perturbation regions}
\label{app:boussinesq_regions}

The Boussinesq perturbation masks are defined in the model's non-dimensional
latitude coordinate $x\in[0,5]$, where $x=0$ corresponds to $90°\text{S}$ and
$x=5$ corresponds to $90°\text{N}$. The North Atlantic mask uses
$x\in[130/36,5]$ and the Tropical Atlantic mask uses $x\in[40/36,130/36]$.
These correspond approximately to $40°$--$90°\text{N}$ and
$50°\text{S}$--$40°\text{N}$, respectively. Both masks are surface-intensified
by the same vertical decay profile that enters the model's salinity forcing,
and both are normalized so that the requested perturbation amplitude equals
the mask-weighted box-mean salinity change.

\section{PlaSim-LSG reduced state space}
\label{app:eof}

The three-dimensional reduced state space used to analyse the
PlaSim-LSG trajectories is defined and justified in full in
\citet{Borner2025AMOCGlobalStability} (their Section~III\,D and Fig.~6).  The
variables — meridional salinity gradient SG$_\text{mer}$, vertical
salinity gradient SG$_\text{vert}$, and deep North Atlantic salinity
anomaly $S'_\text{deep}$ — were derived by EOF analysis of the
Atlantic zonally-averaged salinity field from $20\,000$ years of edge-
tracking simulations at $360\,\text{ppm}$.  The leading two EOFs reveal
a meridional salinity dipole in the upper $1000\,\text{m}$ and a
vertical dipole in the North Atlantic, respectively.  The three
variables chosen are linear functions of the full salinity field that
closely align with these dominant modes while remaining physically
interpretable and directly computable from any spatially resolved ocean
model, facilitating the inter-model comparisons pursued in the present
study.
